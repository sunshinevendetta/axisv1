Grime is a genre of electronic music that emerged in the early 2000s in East London. It is characterized by its fast tempo, typically around 140 BPM, and its aggressive, raw sound. Grime fuses elements of UK garage, drum and bass, and dancehall, with a strong influence from hip-hop. The genre's distinctive sound is often driven by harsh, minimalistic beats, heavy basslines, and sharp, syncopated rhythms.

The roots of grime can be traced back to the UK garage scene of the late 1990s. As UK garage evolved, some producers and MCs began to experiment with darker, harder sounds, moving away from the smoother, more soulful elements of garage. These early experiments laid the groundwork for what would become grime.

Dizzee Rascal is one of the genre's most influential figures. His debut album, *Boy in da Corner* (2003), is widely regarded as a cornerstone of grime, blending rapid-fire lyrics with harsh, electronic beats. Other key artists from grime's early days include Wiley, often referred to as the "Godfather of Grime," who was instrumental in shaping the genre with tracks like "Eskimo" and "Wot Do U Call It?," and MCs like Kano and Skepta, who helped bring grime to a wider audience.

Grime is also known for its lyrical content, which often addresses the realities of life in urban London, including themes of poverty, violence, and social struggle. The genre's raw and unfiltered approach to these topics has made it a powerful voice for a generation of young people.

As grime grew in popularity, it began to gain recognition beyond the UK, influencing various other genres and attracting international attention. By the late 2000s and early 2010s, grime artists were achieving mainstream success, with albums and singles charting both in the UK and internationally.

Despite its mainstream success, grime has maintained its underground roots, with many artists continuing to produce music that stays true to the genre's origins. Grime's influence can be seen in the work of contemporary artists across various genres, from hip-hop to electronic dance music.

Essential tracks:

- "I Luv U" by Dizzee Rascal: A seminal track that helped define grime's sound, showcasing Dizzee Rascal's rapid-fire flow and gritty production.
- "Wot Do U Call It?" by Wiley: A defining track in the genre, addressing the identity of grime and its departure from UK garage.
- "PSYCHO" by Skepta: A track that exemplifies the raw energy and lyrical intensity that grime is known for.
