Acid techno, a subgenre of techno music, emerged in the late 1980s and early 1990s, evolving from the sounds of acid house. The genre is defined by its use of the Roland TB-303 bass synthesizer, which produces the distinctive "squelchy" sound that characterizes acid tracks. This sound is combined with the driving, relentless beats typical of techno, creating a high-energy, dynamic style that has influenced electronic music profoundly.

The roots of acid techno can be traced back to the mid-1980s with the advent of acid house in Chicago. Pioneers like DJ Pierre and his group Phuture, with their seminal track "Acid Tracks," utilized the TB-303 to create a new sonic landscape. This influence quickly spread to Europe, where artists began incorporating these sounds into harder-edged techno tracks.

In the early 1990s, acid techno started to take shape as its own distinct genre. Key early exponents included Richie Hawtin (Plastikman), Aphex Twin, Dave Clarke, and the German duo Hardfloor. These artists pushed the boundaries of the genre, using the TB-303 to craft tracks that were both hypnotic and aggressive, often played at high BPMs (130-150).

London became a central hub for the acid techno movement. The city's underground scene thrived with illegal warehouse parties and raves where acid techno was the soundtrack for a new generation of ravers. The Liberators, D.A.V.E. The Drummer, and Guy McAffer (The Geezer) were prominent figures in this scene, contributing to compilations like "It's Not Intelligent…And It's Not From Detroit…But It's F**king 'Avin It," which highlighted the raw, anarchic spirit of London's acid techno underground.

Acid techno wasn't just confined to the UK. It spread across Europe, finding strongholds in countries like Germany, France, and the Czech Republic, where free parties and squats provided fertile ground for the genre's development. Artists like Hardfloor in Germany and Emmanuel Top in France helped bring the genre to wider audiences.

Despite facing challenges from mainstream electronic music trends, acid techno has experienced periodic resurgences. In recent years, younger artists like Regal and Boston 168 have revitalized the genre, bringing it to new audiences and stages. These modern producers draw from the classic sounds while incorporating contemporary production techniques, ensuring that acid techno remains relevant and dynamic.
